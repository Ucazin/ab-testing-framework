The scenario

We changed the "Pay Now" button from grey to high-contrast green. Did checkout conversion go up by at least 5% relative, without harming revenue per visitor? The decision rule was written before the experiment, not after.

What's inside the framework

  • Power analysis — how many users per arm before you press start. The answer was 48k.
  • SRM detection — chi-square overall and per segment, catching the bug that invalidates entire experiments and that most candidates skip.
  • Pre-period AA test — a placebo check confirming the random assignment didn't show fake differences before the treatment started.
  • CUPED variance reduction — built from scratch, using pre-experiment revenue as the covariate. The technique Microsoft, Netflix, and Booking deploy at scale.
  • Benjamini-Hochberg correction — applied to the 8-segment breakouts so we don't fool ourselves with multiple-comparison noise.
  • An automatic decision document — the script writes the ship/no-ship memo when it finishes.
Why CUPED is the punchline. In this synthetic experiment, CUPED gave θ ≈ 0.014 — a weak covariate, so the confidence interval barely shrank. That's intentional and instructive: CUPED helps when you have a strong covariate, and not before. In production, you'd build a richer one (last-30-day sessions, page-view depth, tenure) and expect a 30-50% CI reduction. The lever is on the analyst.

The decision

  • Conversion: 5.49% → 6.01% (+9.4% relative, p = 0.0068, CI doesn't touch zero).
  • Revenue per visitor: +$0.225 (p = 0.0097, also significant).
  • SRM passes, AA passes, segments are noise after BH.
  • Verdict: SHIP. Projected annualized revenue at 12k visitors/day: ~$984k.
